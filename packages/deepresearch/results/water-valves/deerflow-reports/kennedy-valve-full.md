## 1. Executive Summary

Kennedy Valve Company is a leading manufacturer of fire hydrants, waterworks valves, and fire protection valves, headquartered in Elmira, New York. Founded in 1877 and part of the McWane family of companies since 1988, Kennedy Valve operates a 52.59-acre gray and ductile iron foundry and computerized machining facility. The company serves municipal water utilities, fire protection contractors, and engineering firms across the United States and internationally.

Kennedy Valve is one of several brands under the **McWane Valve & Hydrant Group**, alongside Clow Valve Company, M&H Valve Company, Tyler Union, McWane Ductile, Waterman, and McWane Plant & Industrial [citation:McWane MPI](https://mcwanepi.com/). McWane is a privately held, family-owned enterprise that has invested over $300 million since 1999 in environmental compliance, workplace safety, and facility modernization [citation:McWane Environment](https://www.mcwane.com/responsibility/environment/).

## 3. Historical Timeline

- **1877** — Daniel Kennedy begins manufacturing gate valves in a shop on Gold Street, Lower Manhattan [citation:Company History](https://www.kennedyvalve.com/about/company-history/)
- **1890** — Operations move to Coxsackie, NY; the firm becomes Kennedy Valve Manufacturing Co.
- **1907** — A larger, modern plant is built on a 20-acre site in Elmira, NY (today expanded to 52.59 acres)
- **1920** — Brass foundry added to expand product capabilities
- **1940** — Receives three Maritime Commission awards to manufacture 8 million valves for the Victory Fleet during WWII
- **1962** — Purchased by Grinnell Corp. of Providence, RI
- **1969** — Grinnell acquired by ITT Corp.; Kennedy becomes a wholly owned subsidiary of ITT Grinnell
- **1988** — McWane, Inc. purchases Kennedy Valve from ITT Corp.
- **2007** — Kennedy Valve celebrates 100 years of operations in Elmira, NY
- **2025** — Kennedy Valve wins 1st Place McWane Ergo Cup Award for eliminating manual jackhammering and adding an excavator [citation:McWane Ergo Cup](https://www.mcwaneductile.com/blog/what-is-the-mcwane-ergo-cup/)

## 4. Product Portfolio

Kennedy Valve's product line covers the full spectrum of municipal waterworks and fire protection needs:

### 4.1 Fire Hydrants
- Guardian™ hydrant series — industry-standard dry-barrel hydrants
- Kennedy Guardian iHydrant® — smart hydrants with real-time pressure and temperature monitoring [citation:iHydrant](https://ihydrant.com/)
- Custom configurations based on bury depth, main valve size, and cellular network coverage

### 4.2 Valves for Waterworks
- **Resilient Seated Gate Valves** — primary shut-off valves for water distribution
- **Butterfly Valves** — Kennedy introduced the FIRST UL-listed grooved-end butterfly valve in 1982
- **Check Valves** — for backflow prevention in water systems
- **Plug Valves** — for wastewater and industrial applications
- **Tapping Valves & Sleeves** — for connecting to live water mains

### 4.3 UL-FM Fire Protection Products
- UL-listed and FM-approved gate valves, butterfly valves, check valves, and indicating valves
- Full CAD library available for fire protection engineering design [citation:Fire Protection CAD](https://cadlibrary.kennedyvalve.com/)

### 4.4 iHydrant® Smart Monitoring
The iHydrant system is a feature-rich monitoring platform that provides real-time data on:
- Water pressure fluctuations
- Temperature monitoring
- Flow anomalies and potential leaks
- Theft/vandalism alerts

This IoT-enabled technology helps water utilities move from reactive maintenance to proactive infrastructure management [citation:iHydrant Brochure](https://kennedy2020.tmg04.com/upl/downloads/kenn/products/documents/product-brochure-kennedy-guardian-ihydrant.pdf).

## 5. Manufacturing & Supply Chain Complexity

Kennedy Valve operates a **vertically integrated manufacturing process**:

### 5.1 Foundry Operations
- **Gray iron foundry** — produces cast iron valve bodies, bonnets, and components
- **Ductile iron foundry** — produces higher-strength components for critical applications
- Molten metal processing, sand molding, core-making, and shakeout operations

### 5.2 Machining & Assembly
- Computerized numerical control (CNC) machining centers
- Multi-axis machining for precision valve components
- Assembly lines for hydrant and valve final assembly
- Hydrostatic pressure testing and quality verification

### 5.3 Supply Chain Inputs
- **Raw materials**: Pig iron, scrap steel, ferroalloys, silica sand, resins, core binders
- **Procured components**: Gaskets, seals, fasteners, actuators, position indicators
- **MRO (Maintenance, Repair, Operations)**: Foundry consumables, tooling, machining inserts, lubricants
- **Packaging & logistics**: Pallets, crating, protective materials, freight services

### 5.4 Manufacturing Complexity Factors
- Thousands of active SKUs across hydrant and valve product lines
- Multiple material specifications (gray iron, ductile iron, bronze/brass trim)
- Stringent quality requirements (UL, FM, AWWA certification)
- Seasonal demand fluctuations driven by municipal construction cycles
- Long lead-time raw materials (specialty alloys, castings from external foundries)
- Regulatory compliance (environmental permits for foundry emissions, OSHA safety)

## 8. Procurement & Supply Chain Considerations

### 8.1 Key Procurement Categories
Strategic categories for Kennedy Valve's supply chain:

| Category | Criticality | Supply Chain Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Pig iron & scrap steel** | High | Global commodity pricing, tariffs, transportation costs |
| **Ferroalloys (Si, Mn, Cr, Mg)** | High | Limited domestic suppliers, specialty alloy availability |
| **Foundry consumables (resins, sand, binders)** | Medium | Multiple suppliers available; quality consistency critical |
| **Machined components & castings** | High | Precision requirements; supplier qualification needed |
| **Elastomeric seals & gaskets** | Medium | Material certification for potable water contact |
| **Fasteners & hardware** | Low-Medium | Corrosion-resistant specifications for water service |
| **Industrial equipment & MRO** | Medium | Foundry-specific equipment (ladles, molds, conveyors) |
| **Packaging & logistics** | Medium | Heavy, oversized product transport; LTL/truckload |

### 8.2 Supply Chain Risks
- **Raw material price volatility** — Iron, steel, and alloy markets subject to global fluctuations
- **Single/limited source dependencies** — Some specialty materials may have few qualified suppliers
- **Transportation costs** — Heavy products (valves, hydrants) create high freight costs relative to value
- **Labor availability** — Skilled foundry and machining labor is increasingly scarce
- **Regulatory compliance** — Environmental regulations affect foundry operations and supplier qualifications
- **Certification requirements** — UL/FM compliance requires extensive supplier qualification processes

### 8.3 Strategic Opportunities
- **Supplier rationalization** — Consolidate spend across McWane companies for leverage
- **Vertical integration** — Evaluate internal vs. external casting/machining decisions
- **Sustainability initiatives** — Supplier sustainability scoring for McWane's environmental goals
- **Digital supply chain** — Implement inventory visibility and demand forecasting tools
- **Near-shoring** — Evaluate domestic/regional suppliers for critical materials to reduce lead times

## 11. Summary of Key Insights

1. **Established Legacy**: Kennedy Valve has operated continuously for 149 years, with deep roots in Elmira, NY and a reputation for quality in the waterworks industry.

2. **Vertically Integrated**: The company's own gray/ductile iron foundry and CNC machining center provides control over quality and production scheduling, but also introduces complexity in raw material sourcing and environmental compliance.

3. **Growth Tailwinds**: Federal infrastructure funding (IIJA/IRA) and the national focus on lead service line replacement and drinking water system upgrades are driving robust demand for Kennedy Valve's products.

4. **McWane Ecosystem**: As part of the larger McWane enterprise, Kennedy Valve benefits from shared resources, cross-brand distribution, and collective purchasing power.

5. **Smart Technology**: The iHydrant platform positions Kennedy Valve at the intersection of traditional waterworks and IoT-enabled infrastructure management.

6. **Supply Chain Complexity**: The foundry-based manufacturing model requires careful management of commodity raw materials, certified components, and logistics for heavy finished goods.

7. **Active Community Engagement**: Kennedy Valve and McWane demonstrated strong community investment through the $1.5M trail grant and active participation in industry advocacy on Capitol Hill.
